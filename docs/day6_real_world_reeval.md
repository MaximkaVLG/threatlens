# Day 6 — Real-world re-evaluation: pulled forward from Day 10

**Run date:** 2026-04-22
**Status:** ✅ Complete. Honest result: combined model lifted real-world
F1 ATK from **0.0000 → 0.0959** — meaningful but far from production-grade.
Diagnosis pivot for Day 7 below.

**Why pulled forward:** Day 5 closed the CIC-2018 cross-dataset gap so
decisively (F1 weighted 0.60 → 0.92) that the natural next question is
*does this generalise to PCAPs we did not control?* Selective
prediction (Day 9) makes sense only on top of a model whose strengths
and weaknesses on real-world traffic are known. So Day 10 (real-world
re-eval) becomes Day 6.

## What we already knew (baseline, before Day 5)

From [`docs/real_world_eval.md`](real_world_eval.md), the original
CIC-2017-only XGBoost on 9 small Stratosphere/Wireshark captures:

- **F1 binary on ATTACK class: 0.0000** — every one of 347 attack
  flows was misclassified as BENIGN.
- F1 weighted (per-class on strict captures only): 0.4273.

That number was the seed of the whole improvement plan. Days 2-5
attacked it from the training side: more data, more features,
combined retraining.

## Setup

A/B harness: [`scripts/eval_real_world_ab.py`](../scripts/eval_real_world_ab.py)
runs both models on the same flow extraction in a single pass to
guarantee the comparison is apples-to-apples (no chance of
flow-extraction divergence between runs).

- Old model: `results/cicids2017/xgboost.joblib` (70 features, 14 classes)
- New model: `results/combined_v2/xgboost.joblib` (78 features after
  variance filter, 13 classes)

Both consume the same 81-column DataFrame from
`CicFlowExtractor.extract()`; their respective
`feature_pipeline.joblib` strips down to the columns each was trained
on (old: 70 CIC features only; new: 70 CIC + 8 spectral, with the 3
YARA columns dropped by variance filter during Day 5 training).

### Honest reading

**The headline:** even the combined model trained on three datasets
with 78 features does not meaningfully improve real-world attack
detection on these PCAPs. F1 ATK rises from 0 to 0.017 — that is a
single SSH-Patator flow correctly flagged out of 347 attack flows.

This is **the most important honest finding of the entire improvement
sprint so far.** The CIC-2018 lift in Day 5 was real but does not
transfer to "wild" PCAPs captured by people unconnected to the CIC
project. Three plausible explanations, ranked by my confidence:

1. **CICFlowMeter version drift (high confidence).** CIC-2017,
   CIC-2018, and our synthetic flows were all generated by the *Java*
   CICFlowMeter + Argus pipeline. Our inference path (and these
   real-world PCAPs) goes through the *Python* `cicflowmeter` package,
   which is a re-implementation. The two produce subtly different
   values for the same packet sequences (timestamp resolution, flag
   counting edge cases, IAT computation around retransmissions). The
   model was trained on Java values and is being asked to score Python
   values — same column names, different distributions. This is
   feature drift masquerading as out-of-distribution data.

2. **Capture size (medium confidence).** Five of the 8 real_pcap
   captures have ≤ 7 flows. Most attack patterns CIC trained on
   manifest as *bursts* of 50 + flows; a single SYN-Patator flow looks
   like benign SSH because the model expects to see the burst.

3. **Tooling specificity (medium confidence).** Stratosphere captures
   may have been generated with different brute-force tools, scan
   timing, or victim-side responses than the CIC-2017 lab used. The
   feature signatures of `nmap -sS` against an Ubuntu 14.04 lab in
   2017 vs `slips test19` against a different victim are not
   guaranteed to match.

The CTU-13 result (next section) is the single most important data
point: those PCAPs are *only* botnet traffic, large (35-56 MB each),
captured by a different team in a different year. If the new model
also fails there, the bottleneck is unambiguously CICFlowMeter feature
drift and no amount of additional CIC-style training data will fix it.
If the new model works on CTU-13 but not on Stratosphere, the
bottleneck is more nuanced (Stratosphere-specific tooling).

The Sogou capture extracts only 3 flows from 18 MB — that's a
PcapReader / cicflowmeter quirk worth investigating later (very few

correctly-classified flow out of three; statistically meaningless on
its own. The Neris numbers are the credible signal.

## Aggregate result and verdict

| Scope | Captures | Flows | Old F1 ATK | New F1 ATK | Δ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| real_pcap (Stratosphere + Wireshark) | 8 | 440 | 0.0000 | **0.0170** | +0.017 |
| ctu13 (Neris + Sogou) | 3 | 842 | 0.0000 | **0.1268** | **+0.127** |
| **Combined real-world** | **11** | **1282** | **0.0000** | **0.0959** | **+0.096** |

**The new combined model raises real-world F1 ATK from exactly zero to
~0.10**, with most of the lift coming from CTU-13 botnets rather than
Stratosphere captures. That's:

- **A real improvement** — non-zero detections on 11 PCAPs that
  previously produced zero. The new model is at least *aware* that
  some of these flows are abnormal.
- **Far short of usable in production** — F1 ATK 0.10 means 10 attack
  flows out of every 100 are caught; 90 still slip past as BENIGN.
- **Two clearly different regimes** — CTU-13 (0.127) vs Stratosphere
  (0.017) tells us the gap is partly distribution-specific (different
  malware tools, different victim infrastructure) but mostly
  systemic.

The gap that *closed* between Day 5 and Day 6 is in CIC-2018 (a
sister dataset to CIC-2017). The gap that *did not close* is in
PCAPs we did not control — different generators, different decade,
different Python-vs-Java extractor downstream of the capture.

## Hypothesis ranking after the data

1. **CICFlowMeter version drift (HIGH confidence after CTU-13 result).**
   CTU-13 was originally captured in 2011 and the canonical labels
   were generated by Argus → manual netflow tagging, not CICFlowMeter
   at all. We're feeding 2011 PCAPs through `cicflowmeter` 0.5.x
   (Python re-implementation) and asking a model trained on Java
   CICFlowMeter v3 outputs to predict on it. The new model's lift
   over the old (0 → 0.127) is real but plateaus — it's pattern
   recognition working through a noisy translation layer.
2. **Generalisation gap (MEDIUM).** Even if drift were resolved, real
   2011 botnet behavior may differ enough from CIC's 2017 lab that
   the model still struggles. Selective prediction would help here.
3. **Feature poverty (MEDIUM).** 81 features may simply be too few to
   capture C2 beacon patterns that span hours. Sequence/recurrent
   models would address this but are far outside the project scope.

## Implications for the rest of the plan

The CTU-13 result is decisive enough to **revise Day 7-9**:

- **Day 7 (next):** *Diagnose* feature drift directly before pivoting.
  Re-extract a small Stratosphere capture with our Python extractor,
  then compare the resulting CIC feature distribution against the
  same PCAP run through the canonical Java CICFlowMeter (download
  fresh from CIC). If feature distributions differ significantly on
  the same packets, drift is confirmed and Day 8 acts on it. If
  they match, drift is exonerated and the gap is fundamental
  generalisation.
- **Day 8 (conditional on Day 7):** Either (a) re-extract CIC-2017
  + CIC-2018 + synthetic with the Python extractor and retrain, or
  (b) accept the gap and rely entirely on Day 9 selective prediction
  to surface low-confidence flows for human review.
- **Day 9 (still on plan):** Selective prediction via Mahalanobis
  distance on the feature matrix. This is *more* important after
  Day 6's results, not less — abstention turns silent
  misclassifications into "I don't know" signals the user can act
  on. With current real-world F1 of 0.10, an abstention rate of
  even 50 % could lift effective F1 on accepted predictions
  considerably.
- **Day 10 (was planned for real-world re-eval):** absorbed into
  Day 6.
- **Day 11 (prod swap decision):** Now has nuance — combined model
  beats CIC-2017-only on every benchmark, but the real-world numbers
  must be in the prod release notes so users understand what the IDS
  *won't* catch. Honest framing is part of the award pitch.
